Avoiding Burnout as a Working Student: Mental Health and Self-Care

When you're juggling classes, homework, a part-time job, and off-campus responsibilities, burnout isn't a matter of "if" but "when" - unless you actively prevent it.

Recognizing Burnout Early

Warning signs that you're heading toward burnout:
- Dreading both work AND school (not just one)
- Sleeping through alarms you never used to miss
- Getting sick more often than usual
- Feeling emotionally numb or constantly irritable
- Falling behind in multiple classes simultaneously
- Eating poorly (skipping meals or only eating junk)
- Withdrawing from friends and social activities
- Physical symptoms: headaches, stomach issues, constant fatigue
- Thinking "what's the point?" about school or work
- Using all free time to scroll/zone out because you have zero energy for anything else

The Working Student's Hierarchy of Needs

Priority order when everything feels overwhelming:
1. Sleep (7-8 hours non-negotiable - sacrificing sleep makes everything worse)
2. Basic nutrition (eat something, even if it's not perfect)
3. Class attendance (you can't recover from missed lectures as easily as missed work)
4. Work shift obligations (no-call/no-show can get you fired)
5. Assignments/studying
6. Social life
7. Extra-curricular activities

How Many Hours Should a Student Work?

Research consistently shows:
- 10-15 hours/week: Generally manageable for full-time students, may even improve time management
- 15-20 hours/week: Doable but requires intentional scheduling, grades may dip slightly
- 20-25 hours/week: Risk zone - high burnout potential, grades often suffer
- 25+ hours/week: Not recommended for full-time students unless absolutely financially necessary

If you MUST work 25+ hours:
- Consider reducing course load to part-time (3 classes instead of 5)
- Talk to financial aid about maintaining aid eligibility with fewer credits
- Look into summer/winter courses to make up credits during breaks
- Use your work schedule as evidence when requesting deadline extensions from professors

Practical Self-Care (Not Just "Take a Bath")

Daily habits that actually prevent burnout:
- Morning routine: Even 15 minutes of consistency (coffee, review day's plan) sets your mindset
- Movement: Walk between obligations, stretch during study breaks, anything that's not sitting
- One meal per day that's nutritious and eaten without screens
- 10 minutes of genuine rest: not scrolling, not sleeping, just being quiet
- End-of-day debrief: What went well? What's tomorrow's priority?

Weekly habits:
- One full day (or half-day) with NO work and NO school tasks
- One social activity, even if it's just texting a friend or a 30-minute call
- Meal prep (spending Sunday afternoon cooking saves weeknight stress)
- Review upcoming week: are there any impossible days you need to rearrange?

Communicating Your Limits

With employers:
- "I can't pick up extra shifts during midterms/finals week" is a complete sentence
- Most student-friendly employers expect this - if they don't accommodate school, find a new job
- Ask for your schedule 2+ weeks in advance so you can plan studying around it
- If hours are cut and you need income, ask before assuming - managers may not know your financial needs

With professors:
- Use office hours to explain your situation (most professors were working students too)
- Ask about flexibility BEFORE you're in crisis, not after you've missed 3 assignments
- Many schools have student ombudsman services that can advocate on your behalf
- Disability services can help if anxiety/depression from overwork is documented

With yourself:
- "Good enough" is acceptable for some assignments (not everything needs to be A+ work)
- Saying no to one thing means saying yes to your wellbeing
- Comparing yourself to students who don't work is unfair and unhelpful
- You're playing the game on a harder difficulty setting - give yourself credit

When to Seek Help

Talk to someone if:
- You've been in constant "survival mode" for more than 2-3 weeks
- Anxiety about money/school is preventing you from sleeping
- You're using substances to cope with stress
- You've considered dropping out without a plan
- Physical symptoms of stress aren't going away

Resources available to most students:
- Campus counseling center (usually free or very cheap, limited sessions)
- Crisis text line: Text HOME to 741741
- Online therapy options (BetterHelp, Talkspace offer student discounts)
- Academic advisor (can help restructure your plan to be sustainable)
- Financial aid office (might find resources you didn't know about)
- Dean of Students office (especially if burnout is affecting grades)

Building a Sustainable Semester

The key insight: a schedule that barely works in Week 1 will absolutely collapse by Week 10. Build in slack:
- Don't schedule yourself at 100% capacity. Aim for 70-80% and leave room for the unexpected
- Every 4-5 weeks, take a lighter week (ask for fewer shifts, don't take on extra projects)
- Summer and breaks: either maximize income or maximize rest, don't try to do both
- Check in with yourself monthly: Is this pace sustainable until the end of the semester?
