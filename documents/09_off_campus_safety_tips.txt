How to Stay Safe Living Off-Campus as a Student

Safety tips from students who've lived off-campus, especially for those walking home late from work shifts or studying.

Personal Safety Walking Home Late

- Always tell someone your route and expected arrival time
- Share your live location with a trusted friend or family member (Google Maps, Find My Friends)
- Walk in well-lit areas even if it's a longer route
- Keep headphones off or volume low - you need to hear your surroundings
- Carry a small flashlight or use your phone flashlight in dark areas
- Walk with confidence and purpose - looking lost or distracted makes you a target
- If you feel followed, cross the street. If they follow, go to the nearest open business
- Many campuses offer late-night safety escort services - USE THEM (they exist for this reason)
- Keep your keys in your hand before you reach your door
- Trust your gut - if something feels wrong, it probably is

Apartment Security Basics

- Always lock your doors, even if you're just running to the laundry room
- Don't buzz in people you don't know (common in apartment buildings)
- Get a door stopper or security bar for sliding doors/windows on ground floor
- If your apartment has an old lock, ask your landlord to re-key it (previous tenants may have copies)
- Don't advertise that you live alone on social media
- Keep curtains/blinds closed at night
- A cheap doorbell camera ($30-50) is worth the investment
- Know your neighbors - even just a friendly wave means they'll notice strangers
- Don't leave spare keys in obvious places (under mat, above door frame)
- If you have a package delivered, bring it inside quickly (porch pirates are everywhere)

Digital Safety for Students

- Don't post your daily schedule publicly on social media
- Be careful about location-tagging your apartment in posts
- Use two-factor authentication on everything
- Public WiFi at cafes while studying: use a VPN
- If selling furniture/items on Facebook Marketplace, meet in a public location
- Don't give out your home address to people you just met (use campus address instead)
- Beware of scams targeting students: fake rental listings, "Microsoft support" calls, phishing emails pretending to be your university

Safety for Late-Night Work Shifts

If you work closing shifts at a restaurant, retail, or service job:
- Park under a light or as close to the entrance as possible
- Walk to your car with a coworker if possible
- Have your keys ready before you leave the building
- Check your backseat before getting in your car
- Lock doors immediately once inside
- If taking public transit late, sit near the driver/operator
- Rideshare safety: verify the car make/model and license plate before getting in
- Let your manager know if the parking lot lighting is inadequate

Emergency Preparedness

Every off-campus student should have:
- A basic first aid kit
- Working flashlight with extra batteries
- 3 days of non-perishable food and water
- Phone charger (battery bank for power outages)
- Important documents in one accessible place (ID, insurance cards, lease copy)
- Knowledge of nearest hospital/urgent care route
- Campus police number saved in phone (different from 911)
- Renter's insurance contact info

Dealing with Sketchy Situations

Scenarios students encounter and how to handle them:
- Stranger asks to use your phone: offer to make the call for them or direct them to a business
- Someone knocking on your door unexpectedly at night: don't open it, ask who it is through the door
- A neighbor's apartment being broken into: call police, don't intervene directly
- Feeling unsafe in your area: campus safety offices can sometimes help with relocation resources
- Stalking/harassment: document everything, report to police AND campus safety
- Fire: know two exit routes from your apartment, have a meeting spot with roommates

Building Community for Safety

- Introduce yourself to neighbors - communities watch out for each other
- Join or start a neighborhood watch group chat
- Report broken streetlights to the city (they fix them faster than you'd think)
- Get involved in local community meetings about safety
- Share safety alerts with other students in your building/area
